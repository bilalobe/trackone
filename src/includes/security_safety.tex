% Security and safety net for TrackOne
\section{Security and Safety Net}
\label{sec:security}

This section summarises the threat model, security goals, plausible attacks, critical failures we wish to avoid, and the concrete countermeasures that TrackOne implements or recommends.
The text deliberately focuses on the evidence pipeline: collection and aggregation of telemetry facts into daily blobs, deterministic Merkle batching and day roots, anchoring into the OpenTimestamps (\ac{OTS}) ecosystem (public or stationary calendars), storage of proofs and metadata, and independent verification via \texttt{verify\_cli.py}.
Physical attacks on sensors, network confidentiality, and full \ac{SIL}/IEC 61508 compliance are out of scope for TrackOne; \ac{ADR}-022 frames operational constraints and traceability for deployments.

\subsection{Scope}
We reason about the security of the TrackOne evidence pipeline from ingestion through long-term verifiability.
We ground this section in the relevant \ac{ADR}s: \ac{ADR}-014/\ac{ADR}-022 (stationary calendar and operational policy), \ac{ADR}-019 (chain of custody and replication), \ac{ADR}-024 (anti-replay and ledger vs.
audit separation), and \ac{ADR}-017 (Rust TrackOne-core).
The cryptographic and Merkle logic may be implemented in both Python and Rust; the specification and shared tests define correctness, not any specific language binding.
Specifically included:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Collection and aggregation of telemetry / canonical facts into per-day blobs and Merkle trees (\texttt{facts/}, \texttt{day/*.bin}, \texttt{blocks/}).
  \item Construction and storage of per-day records (\texttt{day/YYYY-MM-DD.json}, \texttt{day/YYYY-MM-DD.bin}) and Merkle roots (\texttt{day\_root}).
  \item Anchoring of \texttt{day\_root} into the \ac{OTS} ecosystem (public calendars or a stationary calendar per \ac{ADR}-014).
  \item Storage and management of \ac{OTS} proofs (\texttt{*.ots}) and \ac{OTS} metadata sidecars (\texttt{*.ots.meta.json}).
  \item Verification tooling and \ac{CI} automation that declares evidence ``verified'' (\texttt{verify\_cli.py}, \ac{CI} tox envs, \ac{ADR}-022 operational checks).
\end{itemize}

Out of scope: physical extraction of device keys, side-channel attacks, network-layer confidentiality (we focus on integrity, authenticity, and non-repudiation), and formal \ac{SIL} certification.

\subsection{Security goals}
We group goals into three categories: Integrity \& Authenticity, Availability \& Robustness, and Operational Trust.

\subsubsection{Integrity and authenticity}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Immutable history: once a day's facts are committed and the day\_root anchored, it should be infeasible to change, reorder, or delete entries without detection by auditors.
  \item Authentic anchors and proofs: an accepted \texttt{day\_root} or \ac{OTS} proof must be provably produced from the intended aggregation and correspond to an on-chain (Bitcoin) commitment at or before the claimed time.
  \item Tamper evidence: modification of \texttt{day.bin}, \texttt{day.json}, headers, \texttt{*.ots}, or \texttt{*.ots.meta.json} must be detected.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Availability and robustness}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Graceful degradation: temporary unavailability of public calendars or a stationary calendar must not corrupt ledger state; such days are recorded as \emph{pending anchor} and surfaced clearly to operators.
  \item Reproducibility and auditability: independent verifiers can recompute a day's root from published facts and validate \ac{OTS} proofs against Bitcoin headers using only published artifacts, schemas, and verifier tools.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Operational trust}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Honest \ac{CLI} semantics: when \texttt{verify\_cli.py} reports success, all relevant checks (Merkle, schema, \ac{OTS} proof) have passed; skipped checks are clearly marked and reflected in exit codes.
  \item Configuration safety: calendar configuration mistakes (mixing test and prod, pointing at malicious calendars) must be hard to perform silently; misconfiguration should be observable in logs/metrics and fail safe.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Plausible attacker models}
We categorize adversaries by capability rather than actor name.
The requirement-level threats (T-*) in Section~\ref{subsec:threat-model} are mapped to these capability-based adversaries (A-*) to analyze countermeasures and failure modes.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{A1 — Network adversary:} can intercept, replay or block traffic to calendar servers and can host arbitrary calendar endpoints (spoofed or malicious calendars).
  \item \textbf{A2 — Storage adversary / insider:} can modify or delete files in storage (facts, day blobs, proofs, meta) but cannot break standard cryptographic primitives.
  \item \textbf{A3 — Malicious operator / misconfiguration:} can change environment variables, \ac{CI} markers, or point verification at incorrect roots; may also disable tests accidentally or intentionally.
  \item \textbf{A4 — Buggy or compromised calendar:} a calendar that returns inconsistent proofs, signs incorrect commitments, or equivocates across clients.
  \item \textbf{A5 — Divergence:} exploits differences between Python and Rust implementations (encoding, integer over/underflow) to craft inputs that verify in one stack but not the other.
  Countermeasure: golden-corpus tests.
  \item \textbf{A6 — Encoding drift:} exploits ambiguity in ``what bytes are committed'' by triggering different serializers (\ac{JSON} vs.\ \ac{CBOR}), aiming to create a root that looks legitimate in one toolchain but is unreproducible.
  \item \textbf{A7 — Availability:} attempts to reduce availability or increase operational load via RF jamming, replay floods, or malformed inputs.
  Goal is downtime, not silent integrity failure.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Critical failures}
We define top-level critical failures (CF-*) whose occurrence we must prevent or detect early.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{CF-1: False positive verification.} \texttt{verify\_cli.py} returns success while the underlying facts or day blob differ from what was anchored.
  This undermines trust.
  \item \textbf{CF-2: Undetected calendar misbehavior.} A calendar equivocates or returns invalid proofs, but verification tooling accepts them, compromising long-term verifiability.
  \item \textbf{CF-3: Loss of proof history.} Proof and meta files are deleted or corrupted, making it impossible to reconstruct the chain of custody for certain days.
  \item \textbf{CF-4: Safety net disabled.} \ac{CI} or \ac{CLI} is misconfigured so critical tests are skipped or stub modes are silently used in production, allowing regressions to land (mitigated by \ac{ADR}-021/\ac{ADR}-022 validation).
\end{itemize}

All design choices and countermeasures are evaluated against these failure modes.

\subsection{Countermeasures and mapping to failures}
The following groups the primary design choices and maps them to attacks and failures they mitigate.

\subsubsection{Cryptographic structure and canonicalization}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Deterministic Merkle trees and stable hashing are the primary integrity primitives (\ac{ADR}-003, \ac{ADR}-006).
  They ensure that any change to \texttt{day.bin} changes the root and will be detected by recomputation.
  \item \textbf{Serialization boundary (ADR-034):} transport encodings are allowed to be compact, but \emph{commitment bytes} are produced only by the TrackOne canonical encoder.
  This prevents accidental serde configuration drift from changing what is committed.
  \item Enforce that \texttt{day.bin} is the canonical artifact used for commitments and stamping; \texttt{day.json} is retained for human inspection but does not define integrity.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{OTS proof sidecars and metadata schema}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Store proofs as sidecar files (\texttt{day/YYYY-MM-DD.bin.ots}) and validated metadata files (\texttt{proofs/*.ots.meta.json}) that include: artifact \ac{SHA}-256, artifact path, \ac{OTS} file path, calendar info, client version and timestamps.
  \item The verifier recomputes artifact \ac{SHA}-256 and compares to the meta file before attempting \ac{OTS} verification; this prevents substitution (AT-2) and aids recovery (AT-5).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Stationary calendar and configuration patterns}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Use a stationary calendar for \ac{CI} and predictable local verification (\ac{ADR}-014): this reduces flakiness and makes equivocation easier to detect in controlled contexts.
  \item Distinguish modes via explicit envvars: \texttt{OTS\_STATIONARY\_STUB} for fast local tests, \texttt{OTS\_CALENDARS} to point at stationary or public calendars, and \texttt{RUN\_REAL\_OTS} to gate slow tests.
  Treat stub mode as non-production and require an explicit opt-in for production runs.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Verification logic and tooling}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \texttt{verify\_cli.py} follows a strict sequence: recompute \ac{SHA}-256 of \texttt{day.bin} => compare to meta => check block header/merkle root => run \ac{OTS} verify.
  Any mismatch aborts with a non-zero exit code.
  \item Exit codes and human-readable messages differentiate between: missing artifact, hash mismatch, \ac{OTS} missing, \ac{OTS} invalid, and calendar unavailability.
  This prevents silent success (CF-1, CF-4).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Testing, CI and schema validation}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \ac{JSON} schemas for block headers, day records, and \texttt{ots.meta.json} are enforced in \ac{CI}.
  Tests include negative mutation tests: deliberately corrupt \texttt{day.bin} or change proof meta and assert verification failures.
  \item Tox/\ac{CI} contracts require that \ac{OTS}-related tests run in an explicit env (e.g., \texttt{ots-cal} or \texttt{real\_ots}) and that PRs touching verification code include tests; missing tests or skipped markers cause \ac{CI} to fail (\ac{ADR}-022 operational policy).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Operational controls and enumerations}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Periodic enumerations and archival scripts check for orphaned \texttt{.ots} files, missing \texttt{.meta.json} pairs, and report anomalies as \ac{CI}/monitoring alerts.
  This helps detect CF-3 and AT-5 early.
  \item Site-specific operator playbooks (\ac{ADR}-022) require a 72-hour pilot, staged rollouts, and explicit thresholds (storage, data loss) that trigger operator intervention rather than silent failure.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Stationary Calendar and Anti-Replay Safety Net}
The combined stationary calendar and anti-replay design form the core of TrackOne's safety net:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Role of a stationary calendar:} A deterministic, locally controlled \ac{OTS} endpoint for \ac{CI} and internal deployments reduces dependence on public calendar flakiness and provides a stable target for weekly ``ratchet'' jobs that exercise real \ac{OTS} flows.
  \item \textbf{Anti-replay + ledger separation:} The replay window ensures each \texttt{(device\_id, frame\_counter)} contributes at most once to the day Merkle tree.
  Replayed or out-of-window frames are recorded in audit logs only, never in \texttt{day.bin} (ADR-024: ledger vs. audit separation).
  \item \textbf{Behaviour under partial failures:} Even with uplink loss or calendar downtime we maintain deterministic \texttt{day.bin} and day roots locally; \ac{OTS} proofs can be backfilled later without rewriting the ledger, and tampering is detectable via meta sidecars and proof verification.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Resilience and Planning}
The security mechanisms double as a resilience scaffold for field operations and \ac{GIS}-based planning:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Safety net layering:} Anti-replay, deterministic Merkle roots, and sidecar proofs ensure outages do not corrupt history.
  This underpins RR-1 and lets operators resume anchoring after link loss without rewriting facts.
  \item \textbf{Peer attestation:} A quorum of operator signatures (RR-4) provides immediate trust for local decisions while public anchors are pending.
  Quorum parameters are site-tuned and surfaced in dashboards.
  \item \textbf{Stationary calendar:} When public calendars are unreliable, a stationary calendar provides predictable proofs (\ac{ADR}-014).
  Artifacts are marked \emph{pending public anchor} (RR-3) to maintain operational momentum.
  \item \textbf{GIS maintenance:} Verification anomalies (hash mismatches, missing proofs) are emitted as tasks with coordinates into the site \ac{GIS} (RR-5), including approach routes and seasonal windows.
  \item \textbf{Flood planning:} Sentinel events from pods near low points trigger \ac{GIS} updates and playbooks (close valves, pause actuation).
  The evidence trail ensures post-event review is audit-ready.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discussion and limitations}
We explicitly acknowledge the remaining risks:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item TrackOne does NOT provide \ac{SIL}-level guarantees; it is a verifiability-first pipeline.
  Automated actuation will require a separate safety case and certification effort.
  \item We rely on Bitcoin as a root of trust; extreme failures would affect proof semantics.
  Mitigations include multi-protocol anchoring and strong meta bookkeeping (\ac{ADR}-015).
  \item Correctness requires disciplined deployment: misconfigured calendars or \ac{CI} treating stubbed \ac{OTS} as production can undermine the safety net (\ac{ADR}-022).
\end{itemize}
